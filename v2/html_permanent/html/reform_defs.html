reform

(rɪˈfɔːm )
Definitions
verb1. (transitive) to improve (an existing institution, law, practice, etc) by alteration or correction of abuses2. to give up or cause to give up a reprehensible habit or immoral way of life3. chemistry to change the molecular structure of (a hydrocarbon) to make it suitable for use as petrol by heat, pressure, and the action of catalystsnoun4. an improvement or change for the better, esp as a result of correction of legal or political abuses or malpractices5. a principle, campaign, or measure aimed at achieving such change6. improvement of morals or behaviour, esp by giving up some vice
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Derived Forms

reformable (reˈformable) adjectivereformative (reˈformative) adjectivereformer (reˈformer) noun

Word Origin

C14: via Old French from Latin reformāre to form again

reform

Powered by COBUILD
(rɪfɔːʳm ) for learners of English
Word forms: reforms , reforming , reformed
Definitions
1. variable nounReform consists of changes and improvements to a law, social system, or institution. A reform is an instance of such a change or improvement. ⇒ The party embarked on a programme of economic reform. ⇒ He has urged reform of the welfare system. ⇒ The Socialists introduced fairly radical reforms.2. verbIf someone reforms something such as a law, social system, or institution, they change or improve it. [V n] ⇒ ...his plans to reform the country's economy. [V-ed] ⇒ A reformed party would have to win the approval of the people.3. ergative verb & verbWhen someone reforms or when something reforms them, they stop doing things that society does not approve of, such as breaking the law or drinking too much alcohol. [V] ⇒ When his court case was coming up, James promised to reform. [V n] ⇒ We will try to reform him within the community.reformed adjective [usu ADJ n] ⇒ ...a reformed alcoholic.4. → See also re-form

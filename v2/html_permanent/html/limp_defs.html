limp1

(lɪmp )
Definitions
verb (intransitive)1. to walk with an uneven step, esp with a weak or injured leg2. to advance in a labouring or faltering mannernoun3. an uneven walk or progress
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Derived Forms

limper (ˈlimper) nounlimping (ˈlimping) adjective, nounlimpingly (ˈlimpingly) adverb

Word Origin

C16: probably a back formation from obsolete limphalt lame, from Old English lemphealt; related to Middle High German limpfen to limp

limp

Powered by COBUILD
(lɪmp ) for learners of English
Word forms: limps , limping , limped , limper , limpest
Definitions
1. verbIf a person or animal limps, they walk with difficulty or in an uneven way because one of their legs or feet is hurt. [V] ⇒ I wasn't badly hurt, but I injured my thigh and had to limp. [V adv/prep] ⇒ He had to limp off with a leg injury.Limp is also a noun. ⇒ A stiff knee following surgery forced her to walk with a limp.2. verbIf you say that something such as an organization, process, or vehicle limps along, you mean that it continues slowly or with difficulty, for example because it has been weakened or damaged. [V adv/prep] ⇒ In recent years the newspaper had been limping along on limited resources. [V adv/prep] ⇒ A British battleship, which had been damaged severely in the battle of Crete, came limping into Pearl Harbor.3. graded adjective & adjectiveIf you describe something as limp, you mean that it is soft or weak when it should be firm or strong. ⇒ She was told to reject applicants with limp handshakes. ⇒ A residue can build up on the hair shaft, leaving the hair limp and dull looking.limply graded adverb & adverb [ADV with v] ⇒ Flags and bunting hung limply in the still, warm air.4. graded adjective & adjectiveIf someone is limp, their body has no strength and is not moving, for example because they are asleep or unconscious. ⇒ He carried her limp body into the room and laid her on the bed. ⇒ He hit his head against a rock and went limp.

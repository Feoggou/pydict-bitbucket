break in

Definitions
Collins English Dictionary
verb (adverb)1. (sometimes foll by on) to interrupt2. (intransitive) to enter a house, etc, illegally, esp by force3. (transitive) to accustom (a person or animal) to normal duties or practice4. (transitive) to use or wear (shoes, new equipment, etc) until comfortable or running smoothly5. (transitive) Australian and New Zealand to bring (new land) under cultivationnoun break-in6. a. the illegal entering of a building, esp by thievesb. (as modifier) ⇒ the break-in plans

break in

Powered by COBUILD
Definitions
COBUILD Advanced British English Dictionary
1. phrasal verbIf someone, usually a thief, breaks in, they get into a building by force. [V P] ⇒ Masked robbers broke in and made off with $8,000. [V P] ⇒ The thief had broken in through a first-floor window.2. → see also break-in3. phrasal verbIf you break in on someone's conversation or activity, you interrupt them. [V P + on] ⇒ O'Leary broke in on his thoughts. [V P] ⇒ Mrs Southern listened keenly, occasionally breaking in with pertinent questions. [V P with quote] ⇒ 'She told you to stay here,' Mike broke in.4. phrasal verbIf you break someone in, you get them used to a new job or situation. [V P n] ⇒ The band are breaking in a new backing vocalist. [Also V n P]5. phrasal verbIf you break in something new, you gradually use or wear it for longer and longer periods until it is ready to be used or worn all the time. [V P n] ⇒ When breaking in an engine, you probably should refrain from high speed for the first thousand miles. [V P n (not pron)] ⇒ Nathan's new running shoes weren't broken in correctly. [Also V n P] →  break

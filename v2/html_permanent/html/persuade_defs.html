persuade

(pəˈsweɪd )
Definitions
Collins English Dictionary
verb (tr; may take a clause as object or an infinitive)1. to induce, urge, or prevail upon successfully ⇒ he finally persuaded them to buy it2. to cause to believe; convince ⇒ even with the evidence, the police were not persuaded

Derived Forms

persuadable (perˈsuadable) or persuasible (perˈsuasible) adjectiveperˌsuadability (perˌsuadaˈbility) or perˌsuasibility (perˌsuasiˈbility) nounpersuader (perˈsuader) noun

Word Origin

C16: from Latin persuādēre, from per- (intensive) + suādēre to urge, advise

Definitions
COBUILD Advanced British English Dictionary
1. verbIf you persuade someone to do something, you cause them to do it by giving them good reasons for doing it. [V n to-inf] ⇒ My husband persuaded me to come. [V n to-inf] ⇒ We're trying to persuade manufacturers to sell them here. [V n to-inf, be V-ed] ⇒ They were eventually persuaded by the police to give themselves up. [Also V n + into, V n]persuader Word forms: persuaders countable noun ⇒ All great persuaders and salesmen are the same.2. verbIf something persuades someone to take a particular course of action, it causes them to take that course of action because it is a good reason for doing so. [V n to-inf] ⇒ The Conservative Party's victory in April's general election persuaded him to run for President again. [V n to-inf] ⇒ It was the lack of privacy that eventually persuaded us to move after Ben was born.3. verbIf you persuade someone that something is true, you say things that eventually make them believe that it is true. [V n that] ⇒ I've persuaded Mrs Tennant that it's time she retired. [V n that] ⇒ We had managed to persuade them that it was worth working with us. [V n + of] ⇒ Derek persuaded me of the feasibility of the idea.persuaded graded adjective & adjective [v-link ADJ, ADJ that] [+ of] ⇒ He is not persuaded of the need for electoral reform. ⇒ I remain persuaded that the decisions we made last year were broadly right.

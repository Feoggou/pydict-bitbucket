remand

(rɪˈmɑːnd )
Definitions
verb (transitive)1. law (of a court or magistrate) to send (a prisoner or accused person) back into custody or admit him or her to bail, esp on adjourning a case for further inquiries to be made2. to send backnoun3. the sending of a prisoner or accused person back into custody (or sometimes admitting him or her to bail) to await trial or continuation of his or her trial4. the act of remanding or state of being remanded5. See on remand
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Derived Forms

remandment (reˈmandment) noun

Word Origin

C15: from Medieval Latin remandāre to send back word, from Latin re- + mandāre to command, confine; see mandate

remand

Powered by COBUILD
(rɪmɑːnd , -mænd) for learners of English
Word forms: remands , remanding , remanded
Definitions
1. verb [usu passive]If a person who is accused of a crime is remanded in custody or on bail, they are told to return to the court at a later date, when their trial will take place. [be V-ed prep] ⇒ Carter was remanded in custody for seven days. [be V-ed prep] ⇒ Both were remanded on bail by Wrexham magistrates until March 24.2. uncount noun [oft N n] [on N]Remand is used to refer to the process of remanding someone in custody or on bail, or to the period of time until their trial begins. ⇒ The remand hearing is often over in three minutes. ⇒ This will mean more remand prisoners being held in police cells. ⇒ She has already served a year on remand.

pull up

Definitions
Collins English Dictionary
verb (adverb)1. (transitive) to remove by the roots2. (often foll by with or on) to move level (with) or ahead (of) or cause to move level (with) or ahead (of), esp in a race3. to stop4. (transitive) to rebukenoun pull-up5. an exercise in which the body is raised up by the arms pulling on a horizontal bar fixed above the head6. British old-fashioned a roadside café

pull-up

(ˈpʊlˌʌp)
Definitions
Collins English Dictionary
noun British1. old-fashioned another name for pull-in2. gymnastics an exercise in which you hold onto a high bar above your head and pull yourself up towards it ⇒ We'll do some pull-ups. If you do some of these every day your arms will soon be as strong as mine.

pull up

Powered by COBUILD
Definitions
COBUILD Advanced British English Dictionary
1. phrasal verbWhen a vehicle or driver pulls up, the vehicle slows down and stops. [V P] ⇒ The cab pulled up and the driver jumped out.2. phrasal verbIf you pull up a chair, you move it closer to something or someone and sit on it. [V P n] ⇒ He pulled up a chair behind her and put his chin on her shoulder. [Also V n P]3. ergative phrasal verbIf you pull up or if something pulls you up, you suddenly stop what you are doing. [V P] ⇒ Suddenly, he pulled up sharply in his stride, and fell to the floor. [V n P] ⇒ She recognized at the same instant Rachel's presence. It pulled her up short.4. phrasal verbIf someone pulls you up or if you pull yourself up, you improve your situation or your skill at something. [V n P] ⇒ We had a very good mathematics mistress who pulled me up. [V pron-refl P] ⇒ He was made redundant and now he's trying to pull himself up again. →  pull

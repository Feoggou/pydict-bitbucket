fray1

(freɪ )
Definitions
noun1. a noisy quarrel2. a fight or brawl3. an archaic word for frightverb archaic4. (transitive) to frighten
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Word Origin

C14: short for affray

fray2

(freɪ )
Definitions
verb1. to wear or cause to wear away into tatters or loose threads, esp at an edge or end2. to make or become strained or irritated3. to rub or chafe (another object) or (of two objects) to rub against one anothernoun4. a frayed place, as in cloth
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Word Origin

C14: from French frayer to rub, from Latin fricāre; see friction, friable

fray

Powered by COBUILD
(freɪ ) for learners of English
Word forms: frays , fraying , frayed
Definitions
1. ergative verb & verbIf something such as cloth or rope frays, or if something frays it, its threads or fibres start to come apart from each other and spoil its appearance. [V] ⇒ The fabric is very fine or frays easily. [V + at] ⇒ The stitching had begun to fray at the edges. [V n] ⇒ Her washing machine tends to fray edges on intricate designs. [V-ing] ⇒ ...fraying edges in the stair carpet. [V-ed] ⇒ He wore frayed jeans and cowboy shirts.2. ergative verb & verbIf your nerves or your temper fray, or if something frays them, you become nervous or easily annoyed because of mental strain and anxiety. [V] ⇒ Tempers began to fray as the two teams failed to score. [V n] ⇒ This kind of living was beginning to fray her nerves.frayed graded adjective ⇒ Nerves became severely frayed when air traffic problems delayed the flight.3. singular nounThe fray is an exciting or challenging activity, situation, or argument that you are involved in. ⇒ There will have to be a second round of voting when new candidates can enter the fray. ⇒ He would be inspiring young people to get into the political fray.4. →  fray at/around the edges

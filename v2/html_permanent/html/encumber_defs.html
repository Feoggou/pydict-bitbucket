encumber

or incumber (ɪnˈkʌmbə )
Definitions
verb (transitive)1. to hinder or impede; make difficult; hamper ⇒ encumbered with parcels after going shopping at Christmas, ⇒ his stupidity encumbers his efforts to learn2. to fill with superfluous or useless matter3. to burden with debts, obligations, etc
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Derived Forms

encumberingly (enˈcumberingly) or incumberingly (inˈcumberingly) adverb

Word Origin

C14: from Old French encombrer, from en-1 + combre a barrier, from Late Latin combrus, of uncertain origin

(ɪnkʌmbəʳ ) for learners of English

Definitions
1. verbIf you are encumbered by something, it prevents you from moving freely or doing what you want. [V n] ⇒ Lead weights and air cylinders encumbered the divers as they walked to the shore. [be V-ed + with] ⇒ It is still labouring under the debt burden that it was encumbered with in the 1980s.encumbered graded adjective & adjective [v-link ADJ] [+ with/by] ⇒ The rest of the world is less encumbered with legislation. ⇒ I'm sure we all wish to be less encumbered by rules which we think unnecessary and restricting.2. verbIf a place is encumbered with things, it contains so many of them that it is difficult to move freely there. [be V-ed prep] ⇒ The narrow quay was encumbered by hundreds of carts.

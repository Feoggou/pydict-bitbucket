condemn

(kənˈdɛm )
Definitions
Collins English Dictionary
verb (transitive)1. to express strong disapproval of; censure2. to pronounce judicial sentence on3. to demonstrate the guilt of ⇒ his secretive behaviour condemned him4. to judge or pronounce unfit for use ⇒ that food has been condemned5. to compel or force into a particular state or activity ⇒ his disposition condemned him to boredom
Derived forms
condemnable (kənˈdɛməbəl ) adjectivecondemnably (conˈdemnably) adverbˌcondemnation (ˌcondemˈnation) nouncondemner (conˈdemner) nouncondemningly (conˈdemningly) adverb
Word origin
C13: from Old French condempner, from Latin condemnāre, from damnāre to condemn; see damn

Definitions
COBUILD Advanced British English Dictionary
1. verbIf you condemn something, you say that it is very bad and unacceptable. [V n] ⇒ Political leaders united yesterday to condemn the latest wave of violence. [V n + for] ⇒ Graham was right to condemn his players for lack of ability, attitude and application. [V n + as] ⇒ ...a document that condemns sexism as a moral and social evil. [Also V pron-refl]2. verb [usu passive]If someone is condemned to a punishment, they are given this punishment. [be V-ed + to] ⇒ He was condemned to life imprisonment. [V-ed] ⇒ ...appeals by prisoners condemned to death.3. verbIf circumstances condemn you to an unpleasant situation, they make it certain that you will suffer in that way. [V n + to] ⇒ Their lack of qualifications condemned them to a lifetime of boring, usually poorly-paid work. [V n to n/-ing] ⇒ He felt condemned to being alone. [be V-ed to-inf] ⇒ Mark was condemned to do most of the work. [Also V n to-inf]4. verbIf authorities condemn a building, they officially decide that it is not safe and must be pulled down or repaired. [V n] ⇒ State officials said the court's ruling clears the way for proceedings to condemn buildings in the area.5. → see also condemned

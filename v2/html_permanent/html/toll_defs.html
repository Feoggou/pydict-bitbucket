toll1

(təʊl )
Definitions
verb1. to ring or cause to ring slowly and recurrently2. (transitive) to summon, warn, or announce by tolling3. US and Canadian to decoy (game, esp ducks)noun4. the act or sound of tolling
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Word Origin

C15: perhaps related to Old English -tyllan, as in fortyllan to attract

toll2

(təʊl , tɒl )
Definitions
noun1. a. an amount of money levied, esp for the use of certain roads, bridges, etc, to cover the cost of maintenanceb. (as modifier) ⇒ toll road2. loss or damage incurred through an accident, disaster, etc ⇒ the war took its toll of the inhabitants3. Also called: tollage (formerly) the right to levy a toll4. Also called: toll charge New Zealand a charge for a telephone call beyond a free-dialling area
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Word Origin

Old English toln; related to Old Frisian tolene, Old High German zol toll, from Late Latin telōnium customs house, from Greek telōnion, ultimately from telos tax

toll

Powered by COBUILD
(toʊl ) for learners of English
Word forms: tolls , tolling , tolled
Definitions
1. ergative verb & verbWhen a bell tolls or when someone tolls it, it rings slowly and repeatedly, often as a sign that someone has died. [V] ⇒ Church bells tolled and black flags fluttered. [V n] ⇒ The pilgrims tolled the bell.2. countable nounA toll is a small sum of money that you have to pay in order to use a particular bridge or road.3. countable noun [N n]A toll road or toll bridge is a road or bridge where you have to pay in order to use it.4. countable noun [usu sing]A toll is a total number of deaths, accidents, or disasters that occur in a particular period of time. [journalism] ⇒ There are fears that the casualty toll may be higher. ⇒ ...the second highest annual murder toll in that city's history.5. → See also death toll6. →  take its toll

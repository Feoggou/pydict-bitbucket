bother

(ˈbɒðə )
Definitions
Collins English Dictionary
verb1. (transitive) to give annoyance, pain, or trouble to; irritate ⇒ his bad leg is bothering him again2. (transitive) to trouble (a person) by repeatedly disturbing; pester ⇒ stop bothering your father!3. (intransitive) to take the time or trouble; concern oneself ⇒ don't bother to come with me4. (transitive) to make (a person) alarmed or confused ⇒ the thought of her husband's return clearly bothered hernoun5. a state of worry, trouble, or confusion6. a person or thing that causes fuss, trouble, or annoyance7. informal a disturbance or fight; trouble (esp in the phrase a spot of bother)exclamation8. mainly British an exclamation of slight annoyance
Word origin
C18: perhaps from Irish Gaelic bodhar deaf, vexed; compare Irish Gaelic buairim I vex

Definitions
COBUILD Advanced British English Dictionary
1. verbIf you do not bother to do something or if you do not bother with it, you do not do it, consider it, or use it because you think it is unnecessary or because you are too lazy. [V to-inf] ⇒ Lots of people don't bother to go through a marriage ceremony these days. [V v-ing] ⇒ Most of the papers didn't even bother reporting it. [V] ⇒ Nothing I do makes any difference anyway, so why bother? [V + with/about] ⇒ ...and he does not bother with a helmet either.2. uncount noun [oft a N]Bother means trouble or difficulty. You can also use bother to refer to an activity which causes this, especially when you would prefer not to do it or get involved with it. ⇒ I usually buy sliced bread–it's less bother. ⇒ The courts take too long and going to the police is a bother. ⇒ Most men hate the bother of shaving.3. uncount nounYou use bother to refer to serious trouble, usually when you want to make it sound less serious than it really is. [British, informal, vagueness] ⇒ Vince is having a spot of bother with the law.4. ergative verb & verbIf something bothers you, or if you bother about it, it worries, annoys, or upsets you. [V n] ⇒ Is something bothering you? [V n] ⇒ That kind of jealousy doesn't bother me. [V n that] ⇒ It bothered me that boys weren't interested in me. [V + about] ⇒ Never bother about people's opinions. [Also V n wh]bothered graded adjective & adjective [v-link ADJ] [+ about] ⇒ I was bothered about the blister on my hand. ⇒ I'm not bothered if he has another child.5. verbIf someone bothers you, they talk to you when you want to be left alone or interrupt you when you are busy. [V n] ⇒ We are playing a trick on a man who keeps bothering me. [V n + with/about] ⇒ I don't know why he bothers me with this kind of rubbish.6. →  bother/bother it7. →  can't be bothered8. →  it's no bother9. → hot and bothered

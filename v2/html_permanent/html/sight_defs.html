sight

(saɪt )
Definitions
Collins English Dictionary
noun1. the power or faculty of seeing; perception by the eyes; vision ▶ Related adjectives: optical, visual2. the act or an instance of seeing3. the range of vision ⇒ within sight of land4. range of mental vision; point of view; judgment ⇒ in his sight she could do nothing wrong5. a glimpse or view (esp in the phrases catch sight of, lose sight of)6. anything that is seen7. (often plural) anything worth seeing; spectacle ⇒ the sights of London8. informal anything unpleasant or undesirable to see ⇒ his room was a sight!9. any of various devices or instruments used to assist the eye in making alignments or directional observations, esp such a device used in aiming a gun10. an observation or alignment made with such a device11. an opportunity for observation12. obsolete insight or skill13. See a sight14. See a sight for sore eyes15. See at sight16. See know by sight17. See not by a long sight18. See out of sight19. See set one's sights on20. See sight unseenverb21. (transitive) to see, view, or glimpse22. (transitive)a. to furnish with a sight or sightsb. to adjust the sight of23. to aim (a firearm) using the sight

Derived Forms

sightable (ˈsightable) adjective

Word Origin

Old English sihth; related to Old High German siht; see see1

Definitions
COBUILD Advanced British English Dictionary
1. uncount noun [oft poss N]Someone's sight is their ability to see. ⇒ My sight is failing, and I can't see to read any more. ⇒ I use the sense of sound much more than the sense of sight.2. singular nounThe sight of something is the act of seeing it or an occasion on which you see it. [+ of] ⇒ I faint at the sight of blood. [+ of] ⇒ The sight of him entering a room could flood her with desire.3. countable noun [oft adj N]A sight is something that you see. ⇒ The practice of hanging clothes across the street is a common sight in many parts of the city. [+ of] ⇒ We encountered the pathetic sight of a family packing up its home. ⇒ Among the most spectacular sights are the great sea-bird colonies.4. verbIf you sight someone or something, you suddenly see them, often briefly. [V n] ⇒ The security forces sighted a group of young men that had crossed the border. [V n] ⇒ A fleet of French ships was sighted in the North Sea.5. countable noun [usu pl]The sights of a weapon such as a rifle are the part which helps you aim it more accurately.6. plural nounThe sights are the places that are interesting to see and that are often visited by tourists. ⇒ We'd toured the sights of Paris. [+ of] ⇒ I am going to show you the sights of our wonderful city. ⇒ Once at Elgin day-trippers visit a number of local sights.7. adverb [ADV adj/adv]You can use a sight to mean a lot. For example, if you say that something is a sight worse than it was before, you are emphasizing that it is much worse than it was. [informal, emphasis] ⇒ She's been no more difficult than most daughters and a sight better than some I could mention. ⇒ We weren't doing anything different to what we've always done. We're just doing it a damn sight quicker.8. → see also sighted, sighting9. →  catch sight of someone10. →  at first sight11. →  in sight/within sight/out of sight12. →  in sight/ within sight13. →  to lose sight of14. →  know someone by sight15. →  out of sight, out of mind16. →  on sight17. →  not a pretty sight18. →  set one's sights on something19. →  sight unseen20. → love at first sight

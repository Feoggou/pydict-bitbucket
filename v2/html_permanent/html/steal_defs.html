steal

(stiːl )
Definitions
Collins English Dictionary
verbWord forms: steals, stealing, stole or stolen1. to take (something) from someone, etc without permission or unlawfully, esp in a secret manner2. (transitive) to obtain surreptitiously3. (transitive) to appropriate (ideas, etc) without acknowledgment, as in plagiarism4. to move or convey stealthily ⇒ they stole along the corridor5. (intransitive) to pass unnoticed ⇒ the hours stole by6. (transitive) to win or gain by strategy or luck, as in various sports ⇒ to steal a few yards7. See steal a march on8. See steal someone's thunder9. See steal the shownoun informal10. the act of stealing11. something stolen or acquired easily or at little cost

Word Origin

Old English stelan; related to Old Frisian, Old Norse stela Gothic stilan, German stehlen

Definitions
COBUILD Advanced British English Dictionary
1. verbIf you steal something from someone, you take it away from them without their permission and without intending to return it. [V n] ⇒ He was accused of stealing a small boy's bicycle. [V n + from] ⇒ Bridge stole the money from clients' accounts. [V] ⇒ People who are drug addicts come in and steal. [V-ing] ⇒ She has since been jailed for six months for stealing from the tills.stolen adjective ⇒ We have now found the stolen car.2. verbIf you steal someone else's ideas, you pretend that they are your own. [V n] ⇒ A writer is suing director Steven Spielberg for allegedly stealing his film idea. [V n] ⇒ His team solved the engineering problem by stealing an idea from nature.3. verbIf someone steals somewhere, they move there quietly, in a secret way. [literary] [V adv/prep] ⇒ They can steal away at night and join us. [V adv/prep] ⇒ Leroy stole up the hall to the parlor.4. singular noun [a N]If you describe something as a steal, you mean that it is very good value. [informal] bargain, snip ⇒ At only £13.50, this champagne is a steal.5. → to steal a glance6. → to steal a march on someone7. → to steal the show8. → to steal someone's thunder

lark1

(lɑːk )
Definitions
noun1. any brown songbird of the predominantly Old World family Alaudidae, esp the skylark: noted for their singing2. short for titlark, meadowlark3. (often capital) any of various slender but powerful fancy pigeons, such as the Coburg Lark4. See up with the lark
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Word Origin

Old English lāwerce, lǣwerce, of Germanic origin; related to German Lerche, Icelandic lǣvirki

lark2 - informal

(lɑːk )
Definitions
noun1. a carefree adventure or frolic2. a harmless piece of mischief3. See what a lark!verb (intransitive)4. (often foll by about) to have a good time by frolicking5. to play a prank
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Derived Forms

larker (ˈlarker) nounlarkish (ˈlarkish) adjectivelarkishness (ˈlarkishness) noun

Word Origin

C19: originally slang, perhaps related to laik

lark

Powered by COBUILD
(lɑːʳk ) for learners of English
Word forms: larks , larking, larked
Definitions
1. countable nounA lark is a small brown bird which makes a pleasant sound.2. countable nounIf you say that doing something is a lark, you mean that it is fun, although perhaps naughty or dangerous. ⇒ The children thought it was a great lark. ⇒ The chances are that the thief will be under 21 and doing it for a lark. ⇒ He'd made it seem rather a lark, to be visiting a supposedly haunted house.3. countable noun [the/this n N]You use lark in expressions such as this acting lark and the writing lark to indicate humorously that you think an activity or job is amusing, foolish, or unnecessary. [British, informal] ⇒ He got the part, and eventually decided the acting lark wasn't half bad.► →  lark around

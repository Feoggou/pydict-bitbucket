incubate

(ˈɪnkjʊˌbeɪt )
Definitions
verb1. (of birds) to supply (eggs) with heat for their development, esp by sitting on them2. to cause (eggs, embryos, bacteria, etc) to develop, esp in an incubator or culture medium3. (intransitive) (of eggs, embryos, bacteria, etc) to develop in favourable conditions, esp in an incubator4. (intransitive) (of disease germs) to remain inactive in an animal or human before causing disease5. to develop or cause to develop gradually; foment or be fomented
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Derived Forms

incubation (ˌincuˈbation) nounincubational (ˌincuˈbational) adjectiveincubative (ˈincuˌbative) or incubatory (ˈincuˌbatory) adjective

Word Origin

C18: from Latin incubāre to lie upon, hatch, from in-2 + cubāre to lie down

Definitions
1. verbWhen birds incubate their eggs, they keep the eggs warm until the baby birds come out. [V n] ⇒ The birds returned to their nests and continued to incubate the eggs. [Also V]incubation (ɪnkjʊbeɪʃən ) uncount noun [+ of] ⇒ Male albatrosses share in the incubation of eggs.2. ergative verb & verbWhen a germ in your body incubates or is incubated, it develops for a period of time before it starts making you feel ill. [V] ⇒ The virus can incubate for up to ten days after the initial infection. [Also V n]incubation uncount noun [usu N n] ⇒ The illness has an incubation period of up to 11 days.3. verbIf you say that plans or ideas incubate, you mean that they develop slowly after a lot of thought or discussion. [V] ⇒ ...the fateful decision which had doubtless been incubating in his mind for years.

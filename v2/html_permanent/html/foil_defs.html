foil1

(fɔɪl )
Definitions
verb (transitive)1. to baffle or frustrate (a person, attempt, etc)2. hunting (of hounds, hunters, etc) to obliterate the scent left by a hunted animal or (of a hunted animal) to run back over its own trail3. archaic to repulse or defeat (an attack or assailant)noun4. hunting any scent that obscures the trail left by a hunted animal5. archaic a setback or defeat
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Derived Forms

foilable (ˈfoilable) adjective

Word Origin

C13 foilen to trample, from Old French fouler, from Old French fuler tread down, full2

foil2

(fɔɪl )
Definitions
noun1. metal in the form of very thin sheets ⇒ gold foil, ⇒ tin foil2. the thin metallic sheet forming the backing of a mirror3. a thin leaf of shiny metal set under a gemstone to add brightness or colour4. a person or thing that gives contrast to another5. architecture a small arc between cusps, esp as used in Gothic window tracery6. short for aerofoil, hydrofoilverb (transitive)7. to back or cover with foil8. Also: foliate architecture to ornament (windows) with foils
Collins English Dictionary. Copyright © Harper Collins Publishers

Word Origin

C14: from Old French foille, from Latin folia leaves, plural of folium

foil3

(fɔɪl )
Definitions
nouna light slender flexible sword tipped by a button and usually having a bell-shaped guard

Word Origin

C16: of unknown origin

foil

Powered by COBUILD
(fɔɪl ) for learners of English
Word forms: foils , foiling , foiled
Definitions
1. uncount nounFoil consists of sheets of metal as thin as paper. It is used to wrap food in. ⇒ Pour cider around the meat and cover with foil. ⇒ ...aluminium foil.2. verbIf you foil someone's plan or attempt to do something, for example to commit a crime, you succeed in stopping them from doing what they want. [journalism] [V n] ⇒ A brave police chief foiled an armed robbery on a jewellers' by grabbing the raiders' shotgun. [V n] ⇒ The idea of building a roof terrace was also foiled by the planning authorities.3. countable noun [usu sing]If you refer to one thing or person as a foil for another, you approve of the fact that they contrast with each other and go well together, often in a way that makes the second thing or person seem better or less harmful. [approval] [+ for] ⇒ He thought of her serenity as a foil for his intemperance. [+ for] ⇒ A cold beer is the perfect foil for a curry.4. countable nounA foil is a thin light sword used in fencing, which has a button on its tip to prevent injury.

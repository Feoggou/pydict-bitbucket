safe

(seɪf )

Definitions

Collins English Dictionary
adjective1. affording security or protection from harm ⇒ a safe place2. (postpositive) free from danger ⇒ you'll be safe here3. secure from risk; certain; sound ⇒ a safe investment, ⇒ a safe bet4. worthy of trust; prudent ⇒ a safe companion5. tending to avoid controversy or risk ⇒ a safe player6. unable to do harm; not dangerous ⇒ a criminal safe behind bars, ⇒ water safe to drink7. British informal excellent8. See on the safe sideadverb9. in a safe condition ⇒ the children are safe in bed now10. See play safenoun11. a strong container, usually of metal and provided with a secure lock, for storing money or valuables12. a small ventilated cupboard-like container for storing food13. US and Canadian a slang word for condom

Derived Forms

safely (ˈsafely) adverbsafeness (ˈsafeness) noun

Word Origin

C13: from Old French salf, from Latin salvus; related to Latin salus safety

Definitions
COBUILD Advanced British English Dictionary
1. graded adjective & adjectiveSomething that is safe does not cause physical harm or danger. ⇒ Officials arrived to assess whether it is safe to bring emergency food supplies into the city. ⇒ Most foods that we eat are safe for birds. ⇒ ...a safe and reliable birth control option. ⇒ ...a programme to make Russian nuclear reactors safer.2. graded adjective & adjective [v-link ADJ]If a person or thing is safe from something, they cannot be harmed or damaged by it. OPPOSITE: at risk [+ from] ⇒ In the future people can go to a football match knowing that they are safe from hooliganism. ⇒ On beaches, keep your camera safe from sand by enveloping it inside a plastic bag. ⇒ Crime Prevention Officers can visit your home and suggest ways to make it safer.3. graded adjective & adjective [v-link ADJ]If you are safe, you have not been harmed, or you are not in danger of being harmed. ⇒ Where is Sophy? Is she safe? ⇒ A baby boy is safe after rescue workers pulled him from a 12-foot-deep construction hole. ⇒ He kissed me twice and I realized suddenly that I felt warm and safe.safely adverb [ADV with v] ⇒ All 140 guests were brought out of the building safely by firemen.4. graded adjective & adjectiveA safe place is one where it is unlikely that any harm, damage, or unpleasant things will happen to the people or things that are there. ⇒ Many refugees have fled to safer areas. ⇒ The elimination of all nuclear weapons would make the world a safer place. ⇒ We shall take the treasure away to a safe place.safely graded adverb & adverb [ADV after v] ⇒ The banker keeps the money tucked safely under his bed.5. adjective [ADJ n]If people or things have a safe journey, they reach their destination without harm, damage, or unpleasant things happening to them. ⇒ 'I'm heading back to Ireland again for another weekend.'—'Have a safe journey.' ⇒ ...the U.N. plan to deploy 500 troops to ensure the safe delivery of food and other supplies.safely adverb [ADV with v, ADV adv] ⇒ The space shuttle returned safely today from a 10-day mission. ⇒ Once Mrs Armsby was safely home, she called the police again.6. adjective [ADJ n]If you are at a safe distance from something or someone, you are far enough away from them to avoid any danger, harm, or unpleasant effects. ⇒ I shall conceal myself at a safe distance from the battlefield. ⇒ He thinks he can find a way to vaccinate the elephants from a safe distance.7. graded adjective & adjective [usu v-link ADJ]If something you have or expect to obtain is safe, you cannot lose it or be prevented from having it. ⇒ We as consumers need to feel confident that our jobs are safe before we will spend spare cash. ⇒ Is the National Health Service safe with the Conservative party? ⇒ Rovers made the game safe with a spectacular second goal in the 84th minute.8. graded adjective & adjective [usu ADJ n]A safe course of action is one in which there is very little risk of loss or failure. ⇒ Electricity shares are still a safe investment. ⇒ It might not be safe politically for the President to leave the country.safely adverb ⇒ We reveal only as much information as we can safely risk at a given time.9. graded adjective & adjectiveIf you disapprove of something because you think it is not very exciting or original, you can describe it as safe. [disapproval] ⇒ ...frustrated artists who became lawyers at an early age because it seemed a safe option. ⇒ Rock'n'roll has become so commercialised and safe since punk.10. graded adjective & adjectiveIf it is safe to say or assume something, you can say it with very little risk of being wrong. ⇒ I think it is safe to say that very few students expend the effort to do quality work in school. ⇒ The only safe assumption is that the world's financial markets will have to find solutions themselves.safely graded adverb & adverb [ADV before v] ⇒ If I go to a grocer I know and trust, I can safely assume the eggs will be fresh. ⇒ I think you can safely say she will not be appearing in another of my films.11. graded adjective [usu v-link ADJ, usu ADJ with n]If you say to someone that their secret is safe with you, you are promising not to tell it to anyone. ⇒ Don't worry, Mr Palin, your secret is safe with me.12. countable nounA safe is a strong metal cupboard with special locks, in which you keep money, jewellery, or other valuable things. ⇒ The files are now in a safe to which only he has the key.13. → see also safely14. →  in safe hands / safe in someone's hands15. →  safe as houses16. →  make somewhere safe for something17. →  to play safe18. →  to be on the safe side19. →  it's better to be safe than sorry20. →  safe and sound21. → a safe pair of hands22. → safe in the knowledge
